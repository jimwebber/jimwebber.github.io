Service Re-use

Posted on October 6, 2006 by jim Posted in Uncategorized — No Comments ↓

Reuse is good. Use is better. (unknown)

There’s been a lot of debate on service re-use in amongst the SOA bloggers and Javier has a written good roundup.

Personally I’m intrigued by the notion of service re-use because it is one of the most sophisticated aspects of service-oriented systems, and I’m encouraged by people talking about it since it might be an indicator that our collective level of sophistication is increasing.

Unfortunately most folks implementing an SOA are still coming at it from a technical perspective, and while SOA is about technology to some extent (in delivery), designing for service re-use is most definitely not a technical issue (though building services which can be re-used certainly is).

Let me clarify this by pointing out there are some services that we already take for granted which are re-used. No-one would deny that Salesforce.com has been a successful service, which enterprises can use to drive their customer relationship management processes. Similarly PayPal has been a successful example (at least to its owners!) of processes that enable money transfer between parties. On a more technical bent, Amazon offers services that allow you to reliably store data with their infrastructure being used to manage all of the processes inherent in such a task. There are numerous other examples that I’m pretty sure you’re aware of which offer a set of processes in the form of a re-usable service.

The keyword here is process. Services only become obviously re-usable when they encapsulate a business process and conversely tend to be brittle or overly generic when they are technically focussed (“data access service” is an oxymoron). When a process can be seamlessly ripped out of a business, moved halfway round the world and replaced with a 3rd party implementation, and still enable business continuity then you know you have a re-usable process because others are using that same process (with a different implementation). Your organisation may have no use for such a process to be re-usable, particularly if it encapsulates an entire operational area (how many times would you re-use a payroll process?) but third parties wanting to provide your organisation with services certainly will experience re-use – if their processes give you a competitive edge.

Perhaps we need to start thinking about re-use the other way round. Design services which encapsulate and expose useful processes from your business area. Don’t expose data, don’t expose objects because these are re-use inhibitors. Instead expose something that a business person can understand and see where it can be leveraged around the organisation.

Give the business people who understand the enterprise the capability to perform enterprise architecture, and let the software people get on with building out that vision (with the business, of course). Perhaps then we might stop thinking explicitly about re-use because it will be the norm.
