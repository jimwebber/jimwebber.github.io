Second Languages

Posted on May 9, 2006 by jim Posted in Uncategorized — No Comments ↓

No, this isn’t about whether you should be doing Ruby or Lisp or Scheme (of course you’re already doing all of those fluently, right?), but more a point of a problem for native English speakers like myself. The BBC is running an interesting op-ed piece on why the British youth may never have to learn a second language because of the growing popularity of English as the lingua franca of Europe (and indeed the world).

I come from an odd slice of a generation where European languages were compulsory throughout mandatory secondary schooling which meant most of my peers left school being able to stitch together enough French, German, or even Spanish to get by, and often as was the case of my classmates having a stronger understanding of the mechanics of the foreign language than they ever did of their native tongue.

This notion of compulsory modern languages relieved my peer group of the burden of having to think about whether to learn a language and which language to learn. More often choices were made on the perceived competencies of the teacher rather than the wider applicability of the lanaguage. As if to reinforce that point I studied french under a phenomenally successful teacher (who recently sadly passed on) who could get a bunch of clueless parochial halfwits from the English Midlands to conjugate all manner of French verbs while still maintaining a complete inability to conjugate the verb “to be” in English [*]. This means that my classmates and I can mostly get round France, bits of Belgium and a few other places round Africa, Indo-China and the Carribean that the French colonial period left as its legacy. We’re not fun to converse with in any intellectual capacity but we can often find out from you where the train station is without resorting to pointing and shouting.

But I digress. The point that I’ve been considering for some time is that as a native English speaker it’s tremendously difficult to make a choice of a second language to learn. Should the British youth be thinking about German? No, it’s rather limited in its applicability. French? ditto. Spanish may be more useful especially considering the British liking for Spanish coastal towns, but often English is spoken widely in such areas. What about Mandarin or Cantonese? Both useful and somewhat widespread but from my own experiences in China it seems that many young people and professionals have a good grasp of English. Otherwise I managed to get by with a phrase book and plenty of smiling and gesturing for other situations (with a phone call to my friend Xiao if things went really wrong!).

The point therefore is that any investment in a foreign language other than the world’s lingua franca has potentially less return on investment than you might hope [**]. As a native English speaker I can confidently know that any investment I make in learning a foreign language is likely to be utterly useless to me when I cross the next border (and our planet has a lot of borders). Oddly, the same does not hold true for those whose native tongue is not English. Investing in learning English means you have a portable widely used language spoken as first-language by around 400 million people and as second language by many more than that.

So my question is this: given the factors above, which language should a native English speaker learn, if any? I suspect that the English speakers are just going to have to learn a given language for a specific task and take the hit, or in most cases simply rely on English being widespread. Your thoughts?

[*] In the West Midlands area, the verb to be is conjugated as: I am, You am (sometimes abbreviated to “yowm”), he/her is (I’m not kidding), we am (abbreviation: weem), you am (again “yowm”), they am (abrbeviation: thame). The past and conditional are similarly bizarre.

[**] This is not to discount the joy of learning new stuff!
