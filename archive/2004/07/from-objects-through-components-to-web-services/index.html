From Objects, through Components, to Web Services

Posted on July 16, 2004 by jim Posted in Uncategorized — No Comments ↓

Savas is over at
CalTech at the moment, presenting some of
the WS-GAF work at a

workshop on Service Composition for
the Virtual Observatory
. While Savas was talking to
Jim, the discussion strayed onto

evangelism for
SOAP messages
 versus the ASMX approach (also covered by
Simon Horrell
in a

recent MSDN article
). Subsequently Savas and I had an IM conversation about
the advantages of the message-oriented approach versus the ASMX approach so that
he could go back and convince Jim.

We didn’t come up with anything new on that front since the advantages are
plain to see: you get better control over the format of your messages if you’re
prepared to put the work in. But as so often happens during a technical
discourse (i.e. argument , they’re commonplace between us and now

Savas is publicly
admitting that we have them
!) we did come up with what we think is a useful way
of describing a service – especially for those coming from an object-oriented or
distributed component background.

Part of this thinking comes from the
book “COM and CORBA: Side by Side” (Amazon: [US,

UK
]) by Jason Pritchard. In this (wonderful) book Dr. Pritchard offers the following
description of the evolution of object-oriented technology into (distributed)
component technology:

“Object interfaces represent a contract. As long as the contract is
maintained, the implementation can change without creating side-affects
throughout the rest of the system. … Encapsulation is often viewed as a way to
ease the maintenance problem evidenced in procedural-based systems. …
Implementation reuse through inheritance has also been given the highest
priority in many large system development efforts. This seems like an obvious
winner: More implementation reuse should result in less development time,
improved time to market and reduced costs. … The sad truth is that [large
scale implementation reuse] has proven almost as difficult with-object based
technologies [as with procedural-based technology] (except in those cases that
get all the publicity).”

He goes on to say of component technology that:

“Components carry encapsulation to an extreme level. They do so by exposing
only a public interface. The actual implementation of a component is completely
hidden such that … the implementation language of the component is unknown.
… By using extreme encapsulation, components each live in their own tiny (or
possibly large) universe. The benefit to the outside world is that a properly
designed component can be plugged into a diverse set of clients.”

Which leads to the meat of this blog entry: Web Services. What distinguishes
a service from a component? The fact that
communication with a service is
stateless
 – all the information needed for a service to execute an action is
contained within the message that instigated that action. This is, to my mind, a
much better classification of the otherwise nebulous “services are
coarse-grained” arm waving that I’ve been guilty of in the past (though I’m sure
I’m in good company there).

And what differentiates a Web Service from other kinds of services? Easy:
SOAP.
