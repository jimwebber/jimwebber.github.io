Chinese and Java: Equally Foreign

Posted on June 28, 2005 by jim Posted in Uncategorized — No Comments ↓

I’m back
in China again after two weeks being home in Sydney. I’m typing this from Hong
Kong airport, and for the first time I’m here during the daytime and can
actually see a little bit of the area. As we flew in over the steep lush-green
hills and sparking seas I resolved to come to Hong Kong one of these days and
leave the airport. Alas today it not to be one of those days, after having
travelled since 4.30am (it’s now 7.30pm) Hong Kong isn’t my final destination.

This time around I’m heading towards the coastal city of Xiamen where I’m
going to be working on a large Java-based system (I’ll be working on the Web
Services pieces). While I was back in Sydney I started some architectural
spikes into the system to both understand the architecture and to get back upÂ 
to speed with Web Services on the Java platform. It’s been a long time since I
developed Web Services with Java, in fact not since I left my friends at
Arjuna
in late 2003 to travel have I used anything remotely Java-ish for Web Service
development. I had, until a few weeks ago, become a happy .Net-head and WSE
programmer.

My first task was to get to grips with a development environment. I chose
Eclipse because it is cheaper than IntelliJ insofar as it costs nothing.
Having made that easy decision, I set about learning to work within the constraints of that
environment and trying not to howl
too much about it. I think I’ve adopted the same mentality as companies that use SAP –
you change because it’s less painful than trying to get the tool to change.
After that the architectural spikes were pretty straightforward:

  1. Naive Axis-based “RPC” which I loathed but had the virtue of running out
    of the box;
  2. Less-naive RPC where the methods exposed as a service talk in terms of
    messages rather than parameters. This has the positive benefit of decoupling
    business objects from messages on the wire and reinforces the importance of
    distribution boundaries;
  3. An Indigo-like approach where the Java
    metadata features are used create the equivalent of [MessageContract],
    [MessageHeader], and [MessageBody] to allow programmers to declare the
    format of messages independently of any binding or transfer mechanism. This
    is supported by a very MESTian explicit send message and event-based
    notification of message arrival like
    Savas‘s
    ssdl.exe tool (side note: will the Java platform folks
    please get with the program and give me baked-in events – the copying from .Net
    precedent has been set y’know).

In my heart of hearts I know that option 3 is the best. But I rather
suspect we will go with option 2 since it has the benefit that we won’t have
to write something like Indigo just to get up and running. Furthermore I
suspect that the Alpine folks will be writing something along these lines
and I wouldn’t want to duplicate that effort – is that right
Steve? If not
then I might just have to think of writing something – how about Jimdigo
:-) I can already feel the cease-and-desist engine being warmed up…

The cruel ending to this story happens to be an unfortunate series of events
at the confluence of Virtual PC, a USB disk (to host said Virtual PC
images), a USB hub, and a whole lot of user stupidity. To cut a long story
short I accidentally turned off the hub and totally trashed my Java
development VPC image (backup? ha!). So here I am in 2005 some years older
and wiser than when I left school but I now board this plane to Xiamen and
the first thing I have to say when I arrive is the modern equivalent of “my
dog ate my homework.” Here’s hoping the software learning has stuck even
though I’m still clearly bereft of common sense.
